## 1. Full List of Just-Cause Reasons (Civil Code 1946.2)

**Source:** California Civil Code Section 1946.2, retrieved from California Legislative Information (https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displaySection.xhtml?sectionNum=1946.2.&lawCode=CIV)

### Threshold for Application
Just-cause protections apply after:
- All tenants have continuously and lawfully occupied the property for **12 months or more**, OR
- At least one tenant has continuously and lawfully occupied the property for **24 months or more**

### AT-FAULT Just Causes -- Subdivision (b)(1)

| Sub | Reason | Exact Statutory Language |
|-----|--------|------------------------|
| (A) | Rent default | "Default in the payment of rent." |
| (B) | Material lease breach | "A breach of a material term of the lease, as described in paragraph (3) of Section 1161 of the Code of Civil Procedure, including, but not limited to, violation of a provision of the lease after being issued a written notice to correct the violation." |
| (C) | Nuisance | "Maintaining, committing, or permitting the maintenance or commission of a nuisance as described in paragraph (4) of Section 1161 of the Code of Civil Procedure." |
| (D) | Waste | "Committing waste as described in paragraph (4) of Section 1161 of the Code of Civil Procedure." |
| (E) | Refusal to renew lease | "The tenant had a written lease that terminated on or after January 1, 2020, or January 1, 2022, if the lease is for a tenancy in a mobilehome, and after a written request or demand from the owner, the tenant has refused to execute a written extension or renewal of the lease for an additional term of similar duration with similar provisions, provided that those terms do not violate this section or any other provision of law." |
| (F) | Criminal activity | "Criminal activity by the tenant on the residential real property, including any common areas, or any criminal activity or criminal threat, as defined in subdivision (a) of Section 422 of the Penal Code, on or off the residential real property, that is directed at any owner or agent of the owner of the residential real property." |
| (G) | Unauthorized assignment/subletting | "Assigning or subletting the premises in violation of the tenant's lease, as described in paragraph (4) of Section 1161 of the Code of Civil Procedure." |
| (H) | Refusal of access | "The tenant's refusal to allow the owner to enter the residential real property as authorized by Sections 1101.5 and 1954 of this code, and Sections 13113.7 and 17926.1 of the Health and Safety Code." |
| (I) | Unlawful use | "Using the premises for an unlawful purpose as described in paragraph (4) of Section 1161 of the Code of Civil Procedure." |
| (J) | Employee/agent failure to vacate | "The employee, agent, or licensee's failure to vacate after their termination as an employee, agent, or a licensee as described in paragraph (1) of Section 1161 of the Code of Civil Procedure." |
| (K) | Failure to deliver possession after notice | "When the tenant fails to deliver possession of the residential real property after providing the owner written notice as provided in Section 1946 of the tenant's intention to terminate the hiring of the real property, or makes a written offer to surrender that is accepted in writing by the owner, but fails to deliver possession at the time specified in that written notice as described in paragraph (5) of Section 1161 of the Code of Civil Procedure." |

### NO-FAULT Just Causes -- Subdivision (b)(2)

| Sub | Reason | Description |
|-----|--------|-------------|
| (A) | Owner/family occupancy | Intent to occupy by owner or specified family members for a minimum of 12 continuous months as primary residence. SB 567 added: must move in within 90 days; at least 25% recorded ownership interest required; if owner fails to occupy for 12 months, must offer unit back to tenant at same rent. |
| (B) | Withdrawal from rental market | "Withdrawal of the residential real property from the rental market." (Ellis Act provisions may also apply.) |
| (C) | Government/court order | Compliance with government or court orders relating to habitability or occupancy. Tenant not entitled to relocation assistance if the condition was caused by the tenant's fault. |
| (D) | Demolition or substantial remodel | Intent to demolish or substantially remodel the unit. "Substantially remodel" means replacing/substantially modifying structural, electrical, plumbing, or mechanical systems requiring permits, or hazardous material abatement, that cannot safely accommodate tenant occupancy for 30+ consecutive days. SB 567 added: must provide description of work, expected timeline, copy of permits or contractor agreements, and statement that tenant may request to reoccupy at same terms. |

**Source:** https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displaySection.xhtml?sectionNum=1946.2.&lawCode=CIV

## 2. Notice Periods for Each Reason

### 3-Day Notices (CCP 1161)

**Source:** California Code of Civil Procedure Section 1161 (https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displaySection.xhtml?sectionNum=1161.&lawCode=CCP)

**IMPORTANT -- PENDING LEGISLATION:** SB 436 (2025-2026 session, Sen. Wahab) would extend the rent default notice from 3 days to **14 days** (excluding Saturdays, Sundays, judicial holidays). As of research date, SB 436 has been amended in the Assembly (June 18, 2025). **This bill has NOT yet been enacted. Current law remains 3 days.** Monitor status at: https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/billNavClient.xhtml?bill_id=202520260SB436

| Notice Type | At-Fault Reasons | Description |
|-------------|-----------------|-------------|
| 3-Day Pay or Quit | (b)(1)(A) - Rent default | Must state amount due, name/phone/address of payment recipient, available hours, financial institution info or electronic transfer details. Excludes Saturdays, Sundays, judicial holidays. |
| 3-Day Cure or Quit | (b)(1)(B) - Lease breach | Tenant has 3 days to cure (perform the condition/covenant). If curable, tenant can fix and stay. |
| 3-Day Unconditional Quit | (b)(1)(C), (D), (F), (G), (I) - Nuisance, waste, criminal activity, unauthorized subletting, unlawful use | No cure opportunity. Lease is automatically terminated per CCP 1161(4). |
| 3-Day Quit (context-dependent) | (b)(1)(H) - Refusal of access | Generally 3-day notice; may be curable depending on circumstances. |
| 3-Day Quit | (b)(1)(J) - Employee failure to vacate | Per CCP 1161(1). |
| 3-Day Quit | (b)(1)(K) - Failure to deliver possession | Per CCP 1161(5). |

### 30-Day and 60-Day Notices (Civil Code 1946.1)

**Source:** California Civil Code Section 1946.1 (https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displaySection.xhtml?sectionNum=1946.1.&lawCode=CIV)

| Notice Period | When It Applies |
|---------------|----------------|
| 30-day notice | Tenancies of **less than 1 year**; or property being sold to natural person within 120 days of escrow opening |
| 60-day notice | Tenancies of **1 year or more** (standard for no-fault terminations under 1946.2) |

**Note:** These are the notice periods for no-fault terminations under 1946.2(b)(2). The at-fault reasons use the 3-day notice framework per CCP 1161.

### 90-Day Notices

**SB 567 requirement (effective April 1, 2024):** For owner move-in evictions under (b)(2)(A), the intended occupant must move in **within 90 days** after the tenant vacates. This is NOT a 90-day notice period to the tenant; rather, it is a 90-day deadline for the owner to actually occupy. The notice to the tenant remains 30 or 60 days depending on tenancy length.

**Source:** SB 567 provisions as codified in Civil Code 1946.2 (https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/billNavClient.xhtml?bill_id=202320240SB567)

## 3. Cure Provisions

**Source:** CCP 1161(3) and (4) (https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displaySection.xhtml?sectionNum=1161.&lawCode=CCP)

### Curable Violations (Tenant Can Fix and Stay)

| Violation | Cure Period | Authority |
|-----------|-------------|-----------|
| Non-payment of rent | 3 days to pay (excl. weekends/holidays) | CCP 1161(2) |
| Material lease breach (performable conditions) | 3 days to cure (excl. weekends/holidays) | CCP 1161(3) |

**CCP 1161(3) exact language on cure right:** "Within three days after the service of the notice, the tenant, or any subtenant, or any mortgagee of the term, or other person interested in its continuance, may perform the conditions or covenants of the lease or pay the stipulated rent...and thereby save the lease from forfeiture."

### NOT Curable (Immediate 3-Day Quit)

| Violation | Authority |
|-----------|-----------|
| Nuisance | CCP 1161(4) |
| Waste | CCP 1161(4) |
| Unlawful use of premises | CCP 1161(4) |
| Unauthorized assignment/subletting | CCP 1161(4) |
| Criminal activity | Civil Code 1946.2(b)(1)(F) |

**CCP 1161(3) on non-curable breaches:** "If the conditions and covenants of the lease, violated by the lessee, cannot afterward be performed, then no notice...need be given."

### Ambiguity Flag
- Whether a specific lease violation is "curable" vs "non-curable" can be a judgment call. Courts look at whether the condition can actually be restored. For example, unauthorized pet may be curable (remove pet), but property damage may not be. **Product should not make this determination automatically.**

## 4. Relocation Assistance Requirements

**Source:** Civil Code 1946.2(d) (https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displaySection.xhtml?sectionNum=1946.2.&lawCode=CIV)

### State Law Requirements (for no-fault terminations under (b)(2))

**Amount:** One month's rent in effect when the notice was served.

**Payment Options (owner must choose one):**
- **(A) Direct payment:** Equal to one month's rent, paid **within 15 calendar days** of service of the notice to terminate.
- **(B) Rent waiver:** Waive in writing the payment of rent for the **final month** of the tenancy, prior to the rent becoming due.

**Critical Compliance Requirements:**
- Owner must notify tenant in writing of the right to relocation assistance or rent waiver.
- **"An owner's failure to strictly comply with this subdivision shall render the notice of termination void."**
- If tenant fails to vacate after receiving relocation assistance, owner may recover the payment as damages in unlawful detainer action.

### Exception
Under (b)(2)(C) (government order), if the condition requiring the order was **caused by the tenant**, the tenant is NOT entitled to relocation assistance.

### Local Ordinance Overrides
Local ordinances may require **substantially more** than one month's rent. See Section 7 below. State law is a floor, not a ceiling -- Civil Code 1946.2 explicitly states it does not preempt local ordinances that are "more protective."

## 5. Notice Content Requirements

### Termination Notice Content

**Source:** Civil Code 1946.2(a), (b), (f), (g) (https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displaySection.xhtml?sectionNum=1946.2.&lawCode=CIV)

All termination notices for covered tenancies must:
1. **State the just cause** in the written notice itself (1946.2(a): "an owner shall not terminate a tenancy without just cause, which shall be stated in the written notice to terminate")
2. **Include the specific statutory basis** -- the notice must cite which subdivision of 1946.2(b) applies

### Lease/Addendum Notice Content (Subdivision f)

Required notification text (in **12-point type minimum**):

> "California law limits the amount your rent can be increased. See Section 1947.12 of the Civil Code for more information. California law also provides that after all of the tenants have continuously and lawfully occupied the property for 12 months or more or at least one of the tenants has continuously and lawfully occupied the property for 24 months or more, a landlord must provide a statement of cause in any notice to terminate a tenancy. See Section 1946.2 of the Civil Code for more information."

**Timing:**
- New/renewed tenancies on or after July 1, 2020: Must be in the lease, as an addendum, or as a signed written notice
- Existing tenancies before July 1, 2020: Written notice required no later than August 1, 2020

### Translation Requirements
Per Civil Code 1632, if the lease was negotiated primarily in Spanish, Chinese, Tagalog, Vietnamese, or Korean, notices and contracts must be translated into that language.

**Source:** Civil Code 1632 (https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displaySection.xhtml?sectionNum=1632.&lawCode=CIV)

### 3-Day Notice Specific Content (CCP 1161(2))
For rent default notices:
- Amount of rent due
- Name, telephone number, and address of person to whom rent payment is to be made
- Usual days and hours that person will be available to receive payment
- If applicable: financial institution account information (bank within 5 miles of property)
- If applicable: reference to established electronic funds transfer procedure

### Void Notice Provision
**Civil Code 1946.2(g):** "An owner's failure to comply with any provision of this section shall render the written termination notice void."

## 6. Service Requirements

**Source:** CCP 1162 (https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displaySection.xhtml?sectionNum=1162.&lawCode=CCP)

### Methods of Service (Residential)

| Method | Description | Additional Steps |
|--------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Personal service** | "Delivering a copy to the tenant personally" | None required |
| **Substituted service** | If tenant absent from residence and business: "leaving a copy with some person of suitable age and discretion at either place" | Must ALSO mail a copy |
| **Posting + mailing** | When residence and business cannot be located: "affixing a copy in a conspicuous place on the property, and also delivering a copy to a person there residing" | Must ALSO mail to property address |

### Mail Service Timeline Extension

**Source:** CCP 1013(a) (https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displaySection.xhtml?sectionNum=1013.&lawCode=CCP)

When service includes mailing, additional days are added to the notice period:
- **+5 calendar days** if mailing and address are both within California
- **+10 calendar days** if either place of mailing or address is outside California but within the US
- **+20 calendar days** if either is outside the US

### Fee Prohibition
CCP 1161(6): "A landlord or its agent shall not charge a tenant a fee for serving any notice" described in the statute.

**Source:** CCP 1161 (https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displaySection.xhtml?sectionNum=1161.&lawCode=CCP)

### San Francisco -- Rent Ordinance (Admin Code 37.9)

**Source:** SF Tenants Union (https://sftu.org/justcauses/) -- primary SF Rent Board website was inaccessible.

**Coverage:** As of January 22, 2020, almost all residential units in SF have just-cause eviction protection, including newly constructed buildings (post-1979 units now covered).

**17 Just-Cause Grounds:**

*Fault-Based (1-7):*

### General Note on Local Ordinances
Civil Code 1946.2 explicitly does NOT preempt local ordinances that are **"more protective"** than state law. Many cities impose:
- Additional just-cause grounds
- Higher relocation assistance payments
- Additional procedural requirements (e.g., Rent Board filings)
- Restrictions on owner move-in evictions
- Mandatory mediation or counseling requirements

**Product implication:** The compliance system must check whether a property is subject to any local just-cause ordinance and apply the MORE restrictive requirements (state or local).

## 8. Retaliation Protections

**Source:** California Civil Code 1942.5 (https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displaySection.xhtml?sectionNum=1942.5.&lawCode=CIV)

### Presumption Period
**180 days.** If a landlord takes retaliatory action within 180 days of a triggering event, a rebuttable presumption of retaliation arises.

### Triggering Events
The 180-day presumption period starts from the latest of:
1. Tenant provides notice or complaint about habitability under Civil Code 1942
2. Tenant reports bed bugs or other habitability issues (oral or written)
3. Tenant files a written complaint with a government agency about habitability
4. Government inspection or citation occurs (even without landlord knowledge)
5. A judicial or arbitration proceeding about habitability commences
6. A judgment or arbitration award is issued (if habitability decided against landlord)

### Protected Tenant Actions (Subdivision d)
Landlords cannot retaliate against tenants for:
- Organizing or participating in tenant associations
- Exercising legal rights (broadly defined)

### Prohibited Retaliatory Conduct
- Eviction
- Rent increase
- Reduction in services
- Threats to report tenant to immigration authorities (specifically prohibited)

### Rebuttal
Landlords may act within the 180-day window IF they:
- State legitimate grounds in writing
- Can prove those grounds at trial

### Limitations
- Tenant cannot invoke the presumption more than **once per 12-month period**

### Remedies (Subdivision h)
- Actual damages
- Punitive damages: $100 to $2,000 per retaliatory act (for fraud, oppression, or malice)
- Attorney's fees

### Product Implication
The system should flag if an eviction notice is being generated within 180 days of any known complaint, inspection, or repair request, and warn the user about the retaliation presumption.

## 9. Exemptions from Just-Cause Requirements

**Source:** Civil Code 1946.2(e) (https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displaySection.xhtml?sectionNum=1946.2.&lawCode=CIV)

The following are **exempt** from just-cause eviction requirements under 1946.2:

| # | Exemption Category | Details |
|---|--------------------| --------|
| 1 | Transient/tourist hotel occupancy | As defined in Civil Code 1940(b) |
| 2 | Nonprofit/institutional housing | Nonprofit hospital, religious facility, extended care facility housing |
| 3 | Student dormitories | Owned and operated by institution of higher education or school |
| 4 | Shared facilities with owner | Tenant shares bathroom or kitchen facilities with the owner |
| 5 | Single-family owner-occupied | Owner-occupied single-family residences with max two units/bedrooms rented |
| 6 | Owner-occupied duplex | Two-unit structures where owner occupies one unit as principal residence |
| 7 | New construction (15-year window) | Housing issued certificate of occupancy within previous 15 years |
| 8 | Single-family (non-corporate) | Non-corporate-owned single-family properties meeting specified ownership criteria; owner must provide notice of exemption to tenant |
| 9 | Deed-restricted affordable housing | Units restricted as affordable for low- or moderate-income households |

### Comparison with AB 1482 Rent Cap Exemptions
The exemptions for just-cause (1946.2) and rent caps (1947.12) are largely the same set of categories. However, the specific criteria within each category may differ. **Product should not assume identical exemption analysis.**

### Key Notes on Exemptions
- **15-year rolling window:** The new construction exemption is based on certificate of occupancy date, so buildings age out of this exemption over time.
- **SFH exemption requires notice:** For exemption #8, the owner must provide written notice of the exemption to the tenant. Without this notice, the exemption may not apply.
- **Owner-occupied exemptions (#4, #5, #6):** Require actual owner occupancy -- not just ownership.

## 10. 2025-2026 Amendments and Pending Legislation

### SB 567 (Effective April 1, 2024)

**Source:** SB 567 bill text (https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/billNavClient.xhtml?bill_id=202320240SB567)

Major changes to Civil Code 1946.2:

**Owner Move-In Evictions (b)(2)(A):**
- Intended occupant must live in unit for minimum **12 continuous months** as primary residence
- Owner must move in within **90 days** after tenant vacates
- If owner fails: must offer unit back to tenant at same rent and reimburse moving expenses
- Ownership requires at least **25% recorded ownership interest** (with exceptions for family trusts)

**Substantial Remodel (b)(2)(D):**
- Must provide description of remodel work and expected timeline
- Must provide copy of required permits or contractor agreements
- Must include statement that tenant may request to reoccupy at same terms after completion

**Withdrawal from Rental Market (b)(2)(B):**
- Clarified language: all "rental units at the rental property" must be withdrawn

**Enforcement (h):**
- Private civil action: up to 3x actual damages for willful conduct or oppression
- Public enforcement: Attorney General and local prosecutors may seek injunctive relief
- **"An owner's failure to strictly comply" renders termination notice void**

## 11. Common Procedural Mistakes That Get Notices Thrown Out

**Sources:** DoorLoop legal guide (https://www.doorloop.com/laws/california-eviction-process); CCP 1161 statutory requirements; Civil Code 1946.2(g)

### Notice Defects That Invalidate Evictions

1. **Wrong notice type:** Using a 3-day unconditional quit when the violation is curable (requires cure-or-quit notice instead). Conversely, offering cure for non-curable violations.

2. **Incorrect rent amount on 3-day notice:** Overstating the amount owed (including late fees, utilities, or other charges that are not "rent" under the lease) invalidates the notice. Courts strictly construe the amount.

3. **Missing required information on 3-day pay-or-quit:** CCP 1161(2) requires: amount due, name/phone/address of payment recipient, days/hours available, and bank account or electronic payment info. Omitting any element can void the notice.

4. **Failure to state just cause:** Under 1946.2(a), termination of a covered tenancy without stating just cause in the written notice is void per 1946.2(g).

5. **Failure to provide relocation assistance for no-fault:** Under 1946.2(d), failure to "strictly comply" with relocation assistance provisions renders the notice void. This includes failure to pay within 15 days or failure to provide written notice of tenant rights.

6. **Improper service:** Serving by mail only without first attempting personal service; not mailing a copy when using substituted service; not adding extra days for mail service.

7. **Premature filing:** Filing unlawful detainer before the notice period expires. The 3-day period excludes weekends and judicial holidays. Many landlords miscount.

8. **Wrong party served:** Not serving all adult occupants; not serving subtenants in actual occupation.

9. **Self-help eviction:** Changing locks, removing belongings, shutting off utilities -- all illegal under Civil Code 789.3.

10. **Missing lease addendum notice:** Failure to provide the 1946.2(f) required notice text (rent cap and just-cause information in 12-pt type).

11. **Ignoring local requirements:** Failure to file required notices with local rent boards (SF, LA, Oakland, Berkeley all have additional procedural requirements).

12. **Inadequate documentation:** Not maintaining records of lease, notices, rent payments, or communications.

13. **Retaliation timing:** Filing within 180 days of a tenant complaint without documented legitimate grounds (triggers 1942.5 presumption).

### Product Implication
The system should validate each of these potential defects before generating a notice.

## 12. Unauthorized Practice of Law (UPL) Considerations

### Statutory Framework

**Source:** BPC 6125 (https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displaySection.xhtml?sectionNum=6125.&lawCode=BPC)

**BPC 6125:** "No person shall practice law in California unless the person is an active licensee of the State Bar."

**BPC 6126 Penalties:**
- First offense: Misdemeanor, up to 1 year county jail and/or $1,000 fine
- Repeat offense: Minimum 90 days county jail
- Suspended/disbarred attorneys: Up to county jail or imprisonment per PC 1170(h)
- Penalties are cumulative

**Source:** BPC 6126 (https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displaySection.xhtml?sectionNum=6126.&lawCode=BPC)

### Legal Document Assistants (BPC 6400-6401)

**Source:** BPC 6400 (https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displaySection.xhtml?sectionNum=6400.&lawCode=BPC); BPC 6401 (https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displaySection.xhtml?sectionNum=6401.&lawCode=BPC)

**What LDAs CAN do:**
- Complete legal documents "in a ministerial manner" (typing per client direction)
- Share "general published factual information that has been written or approved by an attorney" about legal procedures and rights
- Make published legal forms accessible to self-represented individuals
- Handle document filing and service at client's direction

**What LDAs CANNOT do:**
- Provide "any kind of advice, explanation, opinion, or recommendation" regarding legal rights, remedies, or strategies
- Select which forms clients should use
- Offer legal opinions or recommendations

**Registration required:** Must register as LDA under BPC 6402 before providing compensated services.

**Sunsets:** January 1, 2030

### BPC 6401 Exemptions Relevant to Software
- Government employees acting in course of employment
- State Bar licensees and their employees/agents
- Licensed real estate brokers/salespersons handling unlawful detainer as parties
- Registered process servers

**Source:** BPC 6401 (https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displaySection.xhtml?sectionNum=6401.&lawCode=BPC)

### The Line Between Tool/Information and UPL

Based on California law and the LDA framework:

**Likely PERMISSIBLE for a software product:**
- Providing general published factual information about eviction law
- Offering blank forms and templates
- Auto-populating forms with user-provided data (ministerial function)
- Calculating statutory deadlines based on user-input dates
- Flagging potential compliance issues (presenting factual requirements, not legal conclusions)
- Providing statutory text and citations

**Likely IMPERMISSIBLE (crosses into UPL):**
- Recommending which type of notice to use based on facts
- Advising whether a particular eviction ground applies to user's situation
- Determining whether an exemption applies
- Recommending legal strategy
- Drafting custom legal language (beyond filling in templates)
- Telling a user whether their facts support a particular cause of action

**Gray Area (proceed with extreme caution):**
- Interactive questionnaires that guide users to specific notice types based on answers (could be seen as "selecting forms" -- prohibited for LDAs)
- Decision trees that recommend specific legal actions
- Compliance checklists that reach conclusions about legality

### Existing Products in Market
Several existing products generate eviction-related documents in California:
- Property management platforms (e.g., DoorLoop, TurboTenant, Avail) offer notice templates
- These typically provide fill-in-the-blank forms with disclaimers
- Most include prominent disclaimers that the product is not providing legal advice
- Some require the user to select the notice type themselves rather than the software recommending one

### Product Risk Mitigation Recommendations
1. **Prominent disclaimers** on every screen: "This is not legal advice. Consult an attorney."
2. **User selects the notice type** -- software should NOT recommend which notice to serve
3. **Present legal requirements as factual information** -- not as advice
4. **Do not make legal conclusions** -- present facts and let user decide
5. **Include attorney referral** -- direct users to seek legal counsel
6. **Consider attorney review option** -- allow users to have generated documents reviewed by a licensed attorney
7. **Monitor AB 2510 and successor legislation** -- California has considered expanding UPL enforcement to software products

> **FLAG:** The boundary between permissible information tools and UPL is not well-defined in California case law for software products. This is a significant legal risk area. Recommend obtaining a formal legal opinion from a California attorney specializing in legal ethics/UPL before product launch.

## Summary of Key Ambiguities and Conflicting Information

1. **SB 436 (3-day to 14-day change):** Currently in Assembly as of research date. If enacted, fundamentally changes rent default notice period. Product must have a mechanism to update quickly.

2. **Local ordinance details:** LA, Oakland, and Berkeley official websites all blocked automated access. Relocation assistance amounts, specific just-cause grounds, and procedural requirements for these jurisdictions require manual verification against current municipal codes. SF data was partially obtained from SFTU.

3. **Curable vs. non-curable violations:** The statute draws a line (CCP 1161(3) vs (4)) but real-world fact patterns often fall in gray areas. Product should NOT make this determination.

4. **UPL boundary for software:** No clear California appellate authority specifically addressing eviction notice software. The LDA framework (BPC 6400) provides some guidance but was not designed for software products.

5. **15-year new construction exemption:** The "rolling window" nature means a property's exemption status changes over time. Product should calculate based on certificate of occupancy date.

6. **SB 567 owner move-in verification:** The 25% ownership interest requirement and 12-month occupancy requirement create compliance tracking obligations that extend beyond the notice itself.

7. **Translation requirements (CC 1632):** If the lease was negotiated in Spanish, Chinese, Tagalog, Vietnamese, or Korean, the notice may need to be in that language. This adds complexity to notice generation.

8. **Mail service day calculations:** CCP 1013 adds 5 calendar days for in-state mailing. Whether this applies to the 3-day notice period under CCP 1161 is subject to case law interpretation. Some courts have held these are separate requirements.
